I could not believe what I was witnessing. Last Tuesday, Anthropic walked out in front of every tech reporter in the world and announced — fifty percent more Claude for you. Headlines wrote themselves. SpaceX compute deal. Free upgrade. Anthropic is doing you a favor.

On the same afternoon — the same day — they buried the opposite news on a help-center page no journalist reads. They cut your background Claude agents by up to forty times. That is not a typo. Forty. Times.

And here is why you need to care, right now, before I show you the proof. If you run any agent script while you sleep — Claude reviewing your pull requests, OpenClaw, Conductor, T3 code, anything that types "claude dash p" in a terminal — starting June 15th, your monthly programmatic usage gets capped at twenty dollars on Pro, a hundred on Max 5x, two hundred on Max 20x. After that, you pay full API rates. The five thousand dollars of effective API value that people have been pulling out of a two hundred dollar Max plan every month? Gone. The agent loops you've been running on a twenty dollar Pro sub? Gone. If you build anything with Claude that doesn't have a human typing into it — this is your bill changing.

That was weasel. That was wrong. And I'm not the first person saying it. Theo Browne — T3 Code creator, five hundred thousand subscribers, the guy whose own product Anthropic explicitly cited in their announcement — canceled his Anthropic plan two days ago and called it, quote, "an attack on open source." Matt Pocock, who literally sells a Claude Code course, said the announcement, quote, "sounds like a bonus" — quote — "why it might not actually be a bonus." Neither of them put the two May 13th announcements side-by-side and named what that pairing actually is. I'm going to. Math, deception, three moves before June 15th. Let's go.

Here's announcement one. Last Tuesday. Anthropic's official news page. Headline — "Higher usage limits for Claude." Claude Code weekly limits up fifty percent through July 13th. They cite the new SpaceX compute deal. Three hundred megawatts of new GPUs. The story gets picked up by 9to5Google, Business Standard, AndroidHeadlines, Pulse2 — all positive. "Anthropic doubles usage limits." "More Claude for everyone." This is the announcement everyone saw.

And here's announcement two. Same day. Anthropic's help center. Not the news page. The support page. Posted by an account called ClaudeDevs, not the main Anthropic handle. Title — "Use the Claude Agent SDK with your Claude plan." Opening line — "paid Claude plans can claim a dedicated monthly credit for programmatic usage." Sounds like a gift, right? It is not. Starting June 15th, every programmatic Claude usage — every Agent SDK call, every "claude dash p" script, every Claude Code GitHub Action, every third-party tool like OpenClaw or T3 code or Conductor — gets capped at twenty dollars a month on Pro. A hundred on Max 5x. Two hundred on Max 20x. No rollover.

Same day. Same company. One announcement makes the front page. The other gets a help-center URL. And the second one is the one that actually changes how much Claude you can run.

Let me back up Theo's number for you, because it's the real headline. Theo Browne, the T3 Code creator, calculated that anyone using Conductor, Zed, or "claude dash p" in CI scripts on a Max plan has effectively had their usage cut by twenty-five to forty times. He didn't pull that number from thin air. The Max 20x plan, the two hundred dollar a month plan, has been quoted as delivering up to five thousand dollars of effective API value every single month. That's the value indie hackers and small teams have been pulling out of that subscription for the last year using the Agent SDK. Starting June 15th, the same Max 20x plan caps your programmatic usage at two hundred dollars of API value. Five grand to two hundred. That is not a fifty percent change. That is a twenty-five times cut at the conservative end, forty times cut at the heavy end, and on Sonnet-heavy workloads the credible math goes as high as one hundred seventy-five times. The fifty percent increase headline that ran the same day — that's not in the same universe of magnitude. That is a different number, on purpose, pointed at a different audience, on the same calendar day.

And here's the part most people miss. The "Extra Usage" toggle — the thing that lets your subscription keep running past the credit cap by spilling into pay-as-you-go API rates — it is off by default. So a lot of people are going to hit the wall in late June without realizing it. Their agent will silently die mid-job at two a.m. on the eighteenth, and they'll find out the next morning when their PR review didn't ship.

Now — and this is the part I really want you to sit with — the framing of the second announcement is doing actual work. Read the ClaudeDevs tweet word by word. Quote — "We have heard your questions about SDK and claude dash p usage sharing your subscription rate limits." That phrasing — "we heard your questions" — is the same language you use when you're delivering a customer-requested feature. Then — "paid Claude plans can claim a dedicated monthly credit." Claim. Dedicated. Credit. Three words that frame this as something you're being given. Not something being capped.

It got so brazen that Anthropic's own Lydia Hallie — she works on Claude Code, she's a real Anthropic employee — posted a tweet trying to clarify the change and her tweet got Community Noted on X within hours. Community Notes require broad consensus across political lines. They don't go up for spin. The note that landed on her tweet said the truth in one sentence — that programmatic usage now draws from a separate twenty-to-two-hundred dollar monthly credit metered at full API rates. When the platform's own consensus system has to correct your developer relations team about your own pricing change, the framing has already failed.

Then notice where the credit change was posted. The fifty-percent-increase announcement went out from the main at-AnthropicAI account and got blasted by the company's PR machine. The credit-pool change went out from at-ClaudeDevs, a smaller developer-facing sub-account. Same day. Different channels. Different audiences. The press never saw the help-center page. The headline writers wrote what they were handed.

This is not a leak. This is not a stumble. This is the standard playbook every API-priced company runs when they need to take something away — you headline the gift, you footnote the cost. Anthropic just did both on the same day. In the same press cycle. That choice is deliberate. Whether you think it's fair is up to you. But pretend it wasn't intentional and you'll get hit by the next one too.

Move to API key with budget alerts before someone else's bill becomes your problem.

Three moves. In order. Don't skip ahead.

So — my take. Two parts. Part one. The cut itself is defensible. The era of squeezing five thousand dollars of API value out of a two hundred dollar plan was always going to end. That was arbitrage. It was never going to last. Anthropic isn't wrong to fix it.

Part two. The way they fixed it is not defensible. This is the standard API-company playbook — headline the gift, footnote the cost. Bury the bad news on a help page on the same day the good news hits the press. Frame the cut as a credit. Use "we heard your questions" language for a change that takes something away. Let your own developer relations person get Community Noted by the platform. This isn't a billing change. This is a marketing strategy. And the novelty here isn't the cut.
